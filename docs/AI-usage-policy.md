# AI Usage Policy Template

> **Purpose:** Give a small team a practical internal policy for everyday AI use in normal business workflows.
> **Status:** Editable starter template. Replace placeholders and examples with your actual operating rules.
> **Audience:** Small EU teams using mainstream AI tools for drafting, research, support, coding, internal operations, and other ordinary business tasks.
> **Important:** This document is an operational baseline, not legal advice, and it does not guarantee compliance with the EU AI Act, GDPR, employment law, or sector-specific rules.

## How to use this document

This template is meant to be adapted, approved internally, and then used. It is not intended to stay as a generic policy file sitting in a folder.

Recommended rollout for a small team:

1. Assign one owner to customize this policy.
2. Replace bracketed placeholders with your actual team details.
3. Cross-check the policy against the tools listed in your AI systems register.
4. Publish the final version where staff can find it easily.
5. Ask staff to acknowledge it in writing or in your HR/workplace tool.
6. Review it at least quarterly or sooner if your tool usage changes materially.

## Why this policy exists

Small teams often adopt AI before they define rules for safe use. That usually creates the same predictable problems:

- staff use different tools with no shared boundary on what data may be entered
- AI output gets treated like a finished answer rather than a draft
- customer-facing material is sent without enough review
- sensitive internal information ends up in public SaaS tools
- nobody can say who approved a tool, who owns it, or when it was last reviewed

This policy is designed to reduce those failures by setting simple rules on:

- approved uses
- prohibited uses
- data handling
- review expectations
- escalation triggers
- ownership and accountability

## 1. Scope

This policy applies to:

- all employees, contractors, founders, interns, and agency staff using AI on behalf of `[Company Name]`
- all approved or tolerated AI tools used for business purposes
- all business workflows where AI is used to create, analyze, summarize, classify, recommend, or automate work

Examples of in-scope use:

- drafting emails, proposals, blog outlines, support responses, or meeting notes
- summarizing documents or calls
- generating research summaries
- creating internal process drafts
- assisting with code, debugging, or technical documentation
- helping with spreadsheet formulas, analysis, or internal reporting

Examples that need specialist review before use and are generally outside the intended fit of this starter kit:

- making employment, credit, insurance, healthcare, legal, or safety-critical decisions with AI support
- using AI for biometric identification, behavioral scoring, or profiling of individuals
- placing a high-risk AI system on the market or putting one into service

## 2. Policy objectives

The objectives of this policy are practical rather than theoretical:

- enable staff to use approved AI tools productively
- reduce avoidable confidentiality, quality, and reputational risks
- make ownership and review expectations explicit
- create records that support later internal review
- keep the team conservative about regulatory claims

This policy is not intended to ban all AI use. It is intended to stop careless, undocumented, or overconfident AI use.

## 3. Roles and responsibilities

### Policy owner

The policy owner is responsible for:

- maintaining this document
- coordinating updates with tool owners and managers
- tracking acknowledgments or training completion
- reviewing incidents and recurring issues

### Tool owner

Each material AI tool or workflow should have a named owner in the AI systems register. That owner is responsible for:

- confirming the business purpose is legitimate
- checking that the listed controls still match reality
- requesting review before expanding a use case materially
- stopping or escalating use if risk increases

### Managers

Managers are responsible for:

- making sure their teams understand this policy
- approving or rejecting exceptions
- ensuring review happens before important external use

### Individual users

Anyone using AI for work is responsible for:

- following this policy
- using only approved tools for approved purposes
- checking output before relying on it
- escalating concerns quickly
- reporting incidents, mistakes, and near misses

## 4. Approved use

The following uses are normally permitted if they follow the rules in this policy and the relevant tool is listed in the AI systems register or otherwise approved.

### Low-friction internal productivity uses

- drafting internal notes, agendas, summaries, and first-pass documents
- brainstorming ideas, options, and outlines
- rewriting text for clarity, structure, or tone
- translation or language simplification
- summarizing long internal materials for faster review

### Team workflow support

- drafting customer support responses for human review
- drafting marketing content for human review before publication
- creating first-pass research summaries for human verification
- generating spreadsheet formulas, SQL, code suggestions, or debugging ideas
- drafting internal policies, SOPs, and templates for manual review

### Conditional approval

The following are allowed only if extra controls are in place:

- customer-facing messaging
- website or social posts
- sales proposals or commercial statements
- HR-related drafts
- automated workflows that trigger downstream actions
- code or configuration changes that may reach production

For these uses, follow the internal review SOP and any team-specific controls.

## 5. Prohibited use

The following uses are prohibited unless the company explicitly creates a separate approved process with specialist input:

- entering highly sensitive, privileged, or specially protected data into external AI tools without explicit approval
- using AI output as the sole basis for employment, promotion, compensation, disciplinary, credit, legal, healthcare, or safety decisions
- representing AI-generated content as verified fact without review
- sending AI-generated output externally without the required human review
- using an unapproved AI vendor for business data
- uploading client confidential information or trade secrets where the contract, DPA, or internal approval does not allow it
- asking AI to imitate a person deceptively or create misleading synthetic content for external use
- using AI to bypass internal security, privacy, legal, or procurement controls

If you need one of these uses for a legitimate business reason, pause and escalate. Do not proceed informally.

## 6. Data handling and confidentiality rules

This section matters more than most teams expect. Many AI mistakes are not caused by bad outputs. They are caused by careless inputs.

### Data categories

Use these practical categories when deciding what may be entered into an AI tool:

| Data category | Examples | Default rule |
|---|---|---|
| Public | Published website text, public product descriptions, public regulations | Usually allowed in approved tools |
| Internal non-sensitive | Meeting notes, draft internal docs, process notes | Usually allowed in approved tools |
| Internal confidential | Financial plans, roadmap details, unreleased commercial terms | Allowed only if the tool/workflow is approved for it |
| Personal data | Customer or employee names, emails, support content, HR records | Allowed only with a clear lawful basis, approved process, and appropriate vendor terms |
| Special category or highly sensitive data | Health data, biometric data, political opinions, union membership, legal privilege, trade secrets | Do not input into external AI tools unless a separate approved process explicitly allows it |

### Practical rules for users

- If you do not know whether a tool is approved for personal or confidential data, assume it is not.
- Minimize prompts. Do not give the model more information than is needed to complete the task.
- Prefer anonymized, redacted, or synthetic examples where possible.
- Never paste passwords, API keys, secrets, production tokens, or credential material into AI tools.
- Do not upload contracts, HR case files, health information, or client-confidential material unless the company has explicitly approved that exact workflow.
- Where possible, remove names, identifiers, and deal specifics before using AI.

### Quick decision test

Do not enter the data if any of the following are true:

- you would hesitate to send it to a third-party vendor by email
- the information is protected by contract, privilege, or a strong confidentiality expectation
- disclosure would materially harm the company, a client, or an employee
- you are relying on guesswork about what the vendor does with the data

## 7. Output use and human review

AI output must be treated as a draft, not as an authority.

Human review is required before AI-assisted output is:

- sent to customers, suppliers, candidates, or other external parties
- published on a website, blog, help center, or social channel
- used in an internal policy, procedure, or decision memo
- used for pricing, legal, HR, or contractual communication
- committed to production code or production configuration
- used to make or materially influence a decision about an identifiable person

Minimum reviewer expectations:

- read the full output, not just the first paragraph
- verify facts, figures, quotes, and references that matter
- remove any unsupported claims or invented details
- confirm the tone and audience fit
- check for confidential or personal data leakage
- take responsibility for the final output

See the internal review SOP for the operational review workflow.

## 9. New use cases and change control

Staff must seek approval before:

- adopting a new AI tool for work
- materially expanding a tool into a new department or new data type
- using AI in a workflow that affects customers, employees, candidates, pricing, contracts, or regulated operations
- connecting an AI tool to internal systems, production data, or automation pipelines

Suggested lightweight approval questions:

1. What is the business purpose?
2. What data will be used?
3. What could go wrong?
4. What human review will happen?
5. Who owns the tool and review date?

If you cannot answer those questions clearly, the use case is not ready.

## 10. Incident and issue reporting

Report the issue immediately to `[Manager / Policy Owner / Security Contact]` if:

- confidential or personal data may have been exposed
- the AI output was materially false, harmful, discriminatory, or misleading
- an unapproved tool was used for work
- a customer complaint or internal concern relates to AI-generated content
- a vendor announces a breach, major security event, or terms change affecting your use

Use the incident log template even for near misses if the lesson is useful. Small teams benefit from a short record of patterns, not just major failures.

## 11. Training and acknowledgment

Recommended minimum training for a small team:

- a 20-30 minute walkthrough of this policy
- examples of permitted and prohibited prompts
- examples of when second-person review is required
- a simple acknowledgment in HRIS, email, or an internal form

Suggested acknowledgment text:

`I have read the AI Usage Policy, understand that AI outputs require human judgment, and will follow the company rules on tool approval, data handling, and review.`

## 12. Exceptions

Any exception should be:

- specific
- time-limited
- documented
- approved by `[Policy Owner]` and `[Relevant Manager / Security Owner]`

Exception log fields to keep:

- date
- requester
- tool and use case
- data involved
- reason for exception
- approver
- expiry date

If exceptions become common, the policy probably needs updating.

## 13. Implementation notes for the buyer

This section is included to make the template easier to deploy in a real small team.

### Good first customization moves

- replace generic tool names with your actual subscriptions
- name one owner for each recurring tool
- add 3-5 concrete examples of approved use from your business
- add 3-5 concrete examples of prohibited use relevant to your data and clients
- define where staff should report incidents

### Common mistakes to avoid

- writing a policy that sounds strict but does not match how the team actually works
- banning everything, which just drives usage underground
- allowing customer-facing AI use without a review rule
- forgetting to define who updates the policy
- claiming the policy makes the company compliant by itself

## 14. Example practical clauses you may adapt

These examples are intentionally conservative.

### Example: customer support drafting

`AI may be used to draft support replies, but a human agent must review and approve the final message before it is sent to a customer.`

### Example: sales and proposals

`AI may assist with proposal structure and first-pass wording, but pricing, commitments, legal terms, and customer-specific statements must be checked manually before external use.`

### Example: coding support

`AI coding assistants may be used for suggestions and scaffolding, but they may not be treated as a substitute for engineering review, security testing, or normal pull request controls.`

### Example: HR boundaries

`AI may assist with drafting generic job descriptions or interview rubrics, but it may not be used as the sole basis for candidate evaluation, ranking, or employment decisions.`
